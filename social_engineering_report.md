Research Report: The Human Factor in Cybersecurity — Social Engineering Attacks

1. Introduction

      While technical defenses like firewalls and encryption have become increasingly sophisticated, the "human element" remains the most vulnerable link in the security chain (IJSDR, 2026). Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of individuals into performing actions or divuulging confidential information. In 2025, human involvement was present in approximately 60% of all data breaches (Sprinto, 2025). This report examines the mechanics of these attacks and provides a framework for organizational defense.

3. Typology of Social Engineering Attacks

   2.1 Phishing (and its variants)
        Phishing remains the most prevalent social engineering tactic, accounting for nearly 90% of identified attacks in some sectors (GOV.UK, 2026).
        Spear Phishing: Highly targeted attacks customized for a specific individual or organization.
        Whaling: A specialized form of spear phishing targeting C-suite executives to authorize large financial transfers (Secureframe, 2025).
        Vishing/Smishing: Attacks conducted via voice calls (vishing) or SMS (smishing) to bypass email filters.

     2.2 Pretexting
        Pretexting involves an attacker creating a fabricated scenario (a "pretext") to establish trust and obtain information. Attackers often impersonate IT auditors, HR personnel, or external vendors (Imperva, 2026). Unlike phishing, which relies on urgency, pretexting often focuses on building a false sense of legitimacy and rapport.

    2.3 Baiting
        Baiting exploits human curiosity or greed. Attackers leave "bait"—such as a malware-infected USB drive labeled "Payroll 2026"—in public areas like office lobbies. Alternatively, digital baiting involves enticing advertisements or "free" software downloads that contain malicious payloads (Fortinet, 2025).


  4. Case Studies and Impact

       3.1 Case Study: MGM Resorts (2023)
          The Attack: Attackers used a Vishing tactic by calling an IT help desk and impersonating an employee after gathering personal details from LinkedIn.
          Impact: The attack paralyzed hotel operations and casino systems for days, resulting in significant financial losses and data exposure (Secureframe, 2025).
      
        3.2 Case Study: Deepfake Financial Fraud (2024)
          The Attack: A multinational energy firm was defrauded of $25 million when attackers used real-time AI deepfake video conferencing to impersonate a company executive during a meeting (Secureframe, 2025).
          Significance: This marked a shift from text-based deception to sophisticated, AI-driven identity spoofing.

5. The Rise of AI in Social Engineering (2025-2026)

     Generative AI has fundamentally changed the threat landscape. Attackers now use Large Language Models (LLMs) to:
        Eliminate Linguistic Errors: AI produces perfectly written emails, removing traditional "red flags" like poor grammar (Security Journey, 2026).
        Scale Personalization: AI can scrape social media to craft thousands of unique, context-aware spear-phishing messages simultaneously.  
        SEO Poisoning: Attackers use AI to generate "helpful" troubleshooting guides that rank highly on search engines but contain malicious instructions (Optimising IT, 2026).

6. Mitigation and Prevention Strategies

   5.1 Technical Controls
        Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Implementation of FIDO2 or hardware-based tokens to prevent credential theft.
        Endpoint Monitoring: Utilizing AI-based behavioral detection to identify unusual user actions that suggest a compromised account (Optimising IT, 2026).
        DMARC/SPF/DKIM: Strict email authentication protocols to prevent domain spoofing.
      
      5.2 The "Human Firewall"
        Contextual Training: Moving away from annual slides to "point-of-failure" training, where employees receive immediate feedback after failing a simulated phishing test.
        Reporting Culture: Establishing a "no-blame" culture where employees are encouraged to report suspicious activity immediately (IJSDR, 2026).

7. Conclusion

   Social engineering continues to evolve by exploiting fundamental human traits: trust, authority, and curiosity. As AI-powered attacks become indistinguishable from legitimate communications, organizations must shift from a "detect and block" mindset to a Zero Trust architecture where no interaction is trusted by default, regardless of its source.
